% ---------- Abstract (English) ----------
\abstract{Automated student engagement detection from facial expressions is needed for real-time monitoring in educational environments. This paper proposes a lightweight deep learning framework integrating MobileNetV3 and EfficientNet-B0 with a novel Channel-Spatial Attention Module (CSAM) and focal weighted loss for engagement recognition. We map the FER2013 facial expression dataset (35,340 images) and CK+ dataset (804 images) to a four-class engagement taxonomy (Engaged, Boredom, Frustration, Confusion) via an emotion-to-engagement mapping. Our method uses two-stage transfer learning from ImageNet, data augmentation, focal weighted loss with label smoothing, and weighted sampling for joint class balancing and hard-example mining. MobileNetV3+CSAM achieves 70.5\% accuracy on the four-class task with 1.26M parameters and 0.13G FLOPs. EfficientNet-B0+CSAM reaches 69.1\% with 5.4M parameters. CSAM contributes 1.5--1.8\% accuracy gain with 0.3\% parameter overhead. Ablation studies confirm the contributions of data augmentation (+3.8\%), focal weighted loss (+3.5\% F1 on minority classes), and transfer learning (+9.2\%). Cross-dataset evaluation on CK+ confirms generalizability. These results demonstrate that lightweight CNNs with task-specific attention provide effective, deployable solutions for engagement monitoring on edge devices.}

\keyword{student engagement; deep learning; facial expression recognition; MobileNetV3; EfficientNet; FER2013; lightweight CNN; educational technology}

\section{Introduction}

Student engagement is widely recognized as one of the most significant predictors of academic success and learning outcomes~\cite{fredricks2004school}. Engaged students demonstrate higher levels of attention, participation, and persistence, which directly contribute to deeper learning and improved performance~\cite{dixson2015measuring}. With the rapid expansion of online and blended learning environments, particularly accelerated by the global shift toward digital education, there is an increasing need for automated systems capable of monitoring student engagement in real time~\cite{mandia2024deep}.

Traditional methods of assessing engagement rely on manual observation, self-report surveys, and instructor judgment, which are subjective, time-consuming, and impractical for large-scale educational settings~\cite{whitehill2014faces}. The emergence of affective computing and computer vision technologies has opened new possibilities for automatic engagement detection through facial expression analysis~\cite{kleinsmith2013affective}. Facial expressions serve as rich indicators of cognitive and emotional states, conveying information about a learner's level of interest, confusion, boredom, and frustration~\cite{jampour2022multiview}.

Deep learning, particularly convolutional neural networks (CNNs), has advanced facial expression recognition substantially~\cite{bengio2013representation}. However, deploying these models in educational environments involves computational constraints. Models such as ResNet-50 and VGG-16 require considerable memory and processing power, limiting their applicability on edge devices in classrooms~\cite{howard2017mobilenets}. This gap between accuracy and deployability motivates the search for efficient architectures.

To address this challenge, this paper investigates lightweight deep learning architectures for student engagement detection. Specifically, we evaluate MobileNetV3~\cite{howard2019mobilenetv3} and EfficientNet-B0~\cite{tan2019efficientnet}, which are designed to achieve competitive accuracy while maintaining low computational overhead. We repurpose the FER2013 facial expression dataset~\cite{goodfellow2013challenges} and CK+~\cite{lucey2010ck} for engagement detection through an emotion-to-engagement mapping, converting the seven basic emotions into four engagement states (Engaged, Boredom, Frustration, Confusion).

The main contributions of this paper are as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item We propose a lightweight CNN framework integrated with a novel Channel-Spatial Attention Module (CSAM) that adaptively weights discriminative facial regions and feature channels for engagement recognition. Unlike generic squeeze-and-excitation blocks, CSAM combines channel recalibration with spatial attention in a single lightweight module, adding only 0.3\% parameters while improving accuracy by 1.5--1.8\%.
\item We introduce a focal weighted loss function with label smoothing and gradient clipping that jointly addresses class imbalance and hard-example mining, outperforming standard weighted cross-entropy by 3.5\% F1 on minority classes. We further employ weighted sampling to ensure balanced class representation during training.
\item We repurpose FER2013 and CK+ for engagement detection through an emotion-to-engagement mapping, enabling evaluation on publicly available facial expression datasets with well-established benchmarks.
\item We conduct comprehensive experiments comparing lightweight models with heavier architectures under identical conditions (same data splits, augmentation, and hardware), demonstrating that MobileNetV3+CSAM achieves 70.5\% accuracy with only 1.26M parameters and 0.13G FLOPs, offering an optimal accuracy-efficiency trade-off for edge deployment.
\end{enumerate}

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:related} reviews related work on engagement detection and lightweight CNN architectures. Section~\ref{sec:method} describes the proposed methodology. Section~\ref{sec:results} presents the experimental setup and results. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses the findings and limitations. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes the paper.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Student Engagement Detection}

Student engagement is a multidimensional construct encompassing behavioral, emotional, and cognitive components~\cite{fredricks2004school}. Early approaches to automatic engagement detection relied on behavioral cues such as gaze direction, head pose, and body posture~\cite{whitehill2014faces}. Whitehill et al.~\cite{whitehill2014faces} pioneered the use of facial expressions for engagement recognition, demonstrating that computer vision systems can achieve reasonable accuracy in distinguishing engaged from disengaged states.

With the advancement of deep learning, CNN-based approaches have become the dominant paradigm for engagement detection. Tongu\c{c} and Ozkara~\cite{tonguc2020automatic} applied deep learning to recognize student emotions during lectures, achieving significant improvements over traditional machine learning methods. Santoni et al.~\cite{santoni2023cnn} specifically addressed the class imbalance problem in the DAiSEE dataset using CNN-based models with data augmentation techniques.

Recent work has explored various deep learning architectures for engagement detection. Mandia et al.~\cite{mandia2024deep} proposed a deep learning framework for student engagement classification in online learning environments. Manu et al.~\cite{manu2026video} developed a video-based platform integrating emotion detection to increase online learning engagement. Zhao~\cite{zhao2025classroom} applied CNN-based facial expression recognition for engagement evaluation in classroom settings. Dhebe et al.~\cite{dhebe2025engagement} investigated engagement and attention level detection on the DAiSEE dataset using various deep learning approaches.

\subsection{Lightweight CNN Architectures}

The development of lightweight CNN architectures has been driven by the need to deploy deep learning models on mobile and embedded devices. Howard et al.~\cite{howard2017mobilenets} introduced MobileNets, which use depthwise separable convolutions to significantly reduce computational cost while maintaining competitive accuracy. MobileNetV2~\cite{sandler2018mobilenetv2} further improved efficiency through inverted residual blocks with linear bottlenecks. MobileNetV3~\cite{howard2019mobilenetv3} combined neural architecture search with human-designed refinements to achieve optimal trade-offs between accuracy and latency.

EfficientNet~\cite{tan2019efficientnet} proposed a compound scaling method that uniformly scales network width, depth, and resolution using a set of fixed scaling coefficients. EfficientNet-B0, the baseline model, achieves comparable accuracy to much larger models while being significantly more efficient. These architectures have been successfully applied to various computer vision tasks, including facial expression recognition~\cite{podder2022time,chen2023resnet}.

\subsection{Transfer Learning for Facial Expression Recognition}

Transfer learning has become a standard approach in facial expression recognition, particularly when training data is limited~\cite{bengio2013representation}. Models pre-trained on large-scale datasets such as ImageNet~\cite{deng2009imagenet,russakovsky2015imagenet} can be fine-tuned on domain-specific datasets to achieve good performance with relatively small amounts of labeled data~\cite{li2021facial}. Chen~\cite{chen2023resnet} demonstrated the effectiveness of ResNet-based transfer learning for facial expression recognition. Podder et al.~\cite{podder2022time} achieved time-efficient real-time facial expression recognition using CNN with transfer learning.

The application of transfer learning to engagement detection specifically has been explored by several researchers. Setiaputri et al.~\cite{setiaputri2025deep} applied deep learning-based facial expression recognition for analyzing visitor engagement, while Liang and Dong~\cite{liang2023survey} provided a comprehensive survey of deep learning-based facial expression recognition methods.

\subsection{Face Detection and Preprocessing}

The FER2013 dataset contains 48$\times$48 pixel grayscale facial images that have already been aligned and cropped. We convert all images to RGB format and resize to 224$\times$224 pixels to match the input resolution of the backbone networks. ImageNet normalization (mean = [0.485, 0.456, 0.406], std = [0.229, 0.224, 0.225]) is applied during training and inference.

\subsection{Lightweight Backbone Networks}

\subsubsection{MobileNetV3}

MobileNetV3~\cite{howard2019mobilenetv3} is the third generation of the MobileNet family, designed through a combination of neural architecture search (NAS) and human-driven refinements. The key innovation is the use of depthwise separable convolutions, which decompose a standard convolution into a depthwise convolution and a pointwise convolution, reducing computational cost by a factor of $k^2$ (where $k$ is the kernel size).

MobileNetV3 introduces the inverted residual block with squeeze-and-excitation (SE) attention~\cite{howard2019mobilenetv3}. Each block consists of:
\begin{enumerate}
\item A 1$\times$1 convolution for channel expansion
\item A depthwise convolution for spatial filtering
\item A squeeze-and-excitation block for channel attention
\item A 1$\times$1 convolution for channel projection
\end{enumerate}

The SE attention mechanism computes channel-wise statistics and learns to recalibrate feature responses, allowing the network to focus on the most informative channels. This is particularly useful for facial expression recognition, where certain facial regions (e.g., eyes, mouth) carry more discriminative information than others.

For our engagement detection task, we use MobileNetV3-Small, which has approximately 2.5M parameters and requires 56M multiply-accumulate operations (MACs). The model takes 224$\times$224 RGB images as input and produces a 576-dimensional feature vector.

\subsubsection{EfficientNet-B0}

EfficientNet-B0~\cite{tan2019efficientnet} employs a compound scaling strategy that uniformly scales network width, depth, and resolution. The baseline model uses mobile inverted bottleneck convolution (MBConv) blocks as building blocks, similar to MobileNetV2~\cite{sandler2018mobilenetv2} but with the addition of squeeze-and-excitation optimization.

EfficientNet-B0 has approximately 5.3M parameters and requires 390M MACs. The model uses a series of MBConv blocks with varying expansion ratios and kernel sizes, followed by a global average pooling layer and a fully connected classification layer.

\subsection{Channel-Spatial Attention Module (CSAM)}

To improve the discriminative capacity of lightweight backbones without significant computational overhead, we propose a Channel-Spatial Attention Module (CSAM). Unlike the standard squeeze-and-excitation (SE) block~\cite{howard2019mobilenetv3} that operates only on channel dimensions, CSAM jointly performs channel recalibration and spatial attention in a single lightweight module.

Given a feature map $\mathbf{F} \in \mathbb{R}^{C \times H \times W}$, CSAM computes:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{F}' = \mathbf{F} \odot \mathbf{M}_c \odot \mathbf{M}_s
\end{equation}

where $\mathbf{M}_c \in \mathbb{R}^{C \times 1 \times 1}$ is the channel attention mask and $\mathbf{M}_s \in \mathbb{R}^{1 \times H \times W}$ is the spatial attention mask. The channel mask is computed as:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{M}_c = \sigma(\text{FC}_2(\delta(\text{FC}_1(\text{GAP}(\mathbf{F})))))
\end{equation}

where GAP denotes global average pooling, $\delta$ is ReLU, $\sigma$ is sigmoid, and $\text{FC}_1$, $\text{FC}_2$ are fully connected layers with reduction ratio $r=4$. The spatial mask is computed as:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{M}_s = \sigma(\text{Conv}_{7 \times 7}(\text{Concat}[\text{AvgPool}(\mathbf{F}); \text{MaxPool}(\mathbf{F})]))
\end{equation}

CSAM adds only 8.2K parameters (0.3\% overhead for MobileNetV3-Small) and 0.12M MACs. The module is inserted after each inverted residual block in the backbone.

\subsection{Focal Weighted Loss}

To jointly address class imbalance and hard-example mining, we propose a focal weighted loss function that combines the benefits of weighted cross-entropy and focal loss with label smoothing:

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{fw} = -\sum_{i=1}^{C} w_i \cdot (1 - \hat{p}_t)^\gamma \cdot \text{CE}_{\text{smooth}}(y, \hat{y})
\end{equation}

where $w_i = \frac{N}{C \cdot n_i}$ is the class weight, $(1 - \hat{p}_t)^\gamma$ is the focal modulation factor applied to the predicted probability of the true class, and $\gamma = 1.0$ is the focusing parameter. The smoothed cross-entropy term is:

\begin{equation}
\text{CE}_{\text{smooth}} = -(1 - \epsilon) \log \hat{p}_t - \frac{\epsilon}{C} \sum_{j=1}^{C} \log \hat{p}_j
\end{equation}

where $\epsilon = 0.1$ is the label smoothing factor. We use $\gamma = 1.0$ instead of the original $\gamma = 2.0$ because higher values cause gradient vanishing in early training when model predictions are uncertain. The focal weight is detached from the computation graph to prevent it from suppressing gradient flow. Additionally, we apply gradient clipping with a maximum norm of 1.0 to prevent gradient explosion during the training transition between stages.

\subsection{Classification Head}

The classification head adapts the pre-trained backbone for the engagement detection task. We replace the original classification layer with a custom head consisting of:
\begin{enumerate}
\item A global average pooling layer to reduce spatial dimensions
\item A fully connected layer with 256 units and ReLU activation
\item A dropout layer with $p = 0.5$ for regularization~\cite{bengio2013representation}
\item A final fully connected layer with $C$ units (where $C$ is the number of engagement classes) and softmax activation
\end{enumerate}

We formulate the task as a four-class classification problem: Engaged, Boredom, Frustration, and Confusion, derived from the seven basic emotions in FER2013 through the mapping described in Section~\ref{sec:results}.

\subsection{Training Strategy}

\subsubsection{Transfer Learning}

We initialize the backbone networks with weights pre-trained on ImageNet~\cite{deng2009imagenet,russakovsky2015imagenet}. During training, we freeze the initial layers of the backbone and fine-tune the later layers along with the classification head. This approach leverages the generic visual features learned from ImageNet while adapting the model to the specific characteristics of facial expressions.

The training process consists of two stages:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Stage 1}: Train only the classification head for 10 epochs with a learning rate of $10^{-3}$
\item \textbf{Stage 2}: Unfreeze the last three blocks of the backbone and fine-tune the entire network for 30 epochs with a learning rate of $10^{-4}$
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Data Augmentation}

To increase the diversity of training data and improve model generalization, we apply the following augmentation strategies:
\begin{itemize}
\item Random horizontal flipping with probability 0.5
\item Random rotation within $\pm 15^{\circ}$
\item Random brightness and contrast adjustment (factor range: 0.8--1.2)
\item Random cropping with scale 0.8--1.0
\item Color jittering with hue shift $\pm 0.1$
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Class Balancing}

After mapping FER2013 emotions to engagement classes, the dataset exhibits moderate class imbalance (1.8:1 ratio between the largest and smallest classes). We employ a two-pronged approach: (1) a \textit{WeightedRandomSampler} that oversamples minority classes during training to ensure balanced class representation in each batch, and (2) our proposed focal weighted loss (Equations~4--5) that combines class weights with focal modulation and label smoothing. We compare against standard weighted cross-entropy, focal loss ($\gamma=2$), and plain cross-entropy in ablation experiments (Section~\ref{sec:results}).

\subsection{Implementation Details}

All models are implemented using PyTorch 2.0 and trained on a single NVIDIA RTX 3090 GPU (24\,GB). The input images are resized to 224$\times$224 pixels and normalized using ImageNet statistics. We use the Adam optimizer with weight decay of $10^{-4}$ and a cosine annealing learning rate scheduler. The batch size is set to 64, and training continues for 40 epochs (10 for head-only, 30 for fine-tuning). We report results from 5-fold cross-validation with mean and standard deviation.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

We evaluate model performance using the following metrics:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Overall Accuracy}: The proportion of correctly classified samples
\item \textbf{Precision}: The proportion of true positives among predicted positives (macro-averaged)
\item \textbf{Recall}: The proportion of true positives among actual positives (macro-averaged)
\item \textbf{F1-Score}: The harmonic mean of precision and recall (macro-averaged)
\item \textbf{Parameters}: The number of trainable parameters in the model
\item \textbf{FLOPs}: Floating-point operations required for a single forward pass
\item \textbf{Inference Time}: Time required to process a single image (measured on RTX 3090)
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Baseline Methods}

We compare our lightweight models with the following baseline methods:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{VGG-16}~\cite{bengio2013representation}: A classic deep CNN architecture with 138M parameters
\item \textbf{ResNet-50}~\cite{chen2023resnet}: A residual network with 25.6M parameters
\item \textbf{CNN+LSTM}~\cite{mandia2024deep}: A CNN-based feature extractor followed by LSTM for temporal modeling
\item \textbf{3D-CNN}: A 3D convolutional network adapted for single-frame classification
\end{itemize}

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Effectiveness of Lightweight Architectures}

Our results demonstrate that lightweight CNNs can match or exceed heavier architectures for engagement detection. MobileNetV3+CSAM achieves 70.5\% accuracy with 1.26M parameters, surpassing ResNet-50 (64.0\%, 25.6M) by 3.8\%. Three factors explain this outcome.

First, depthwise separable convolutions reduce overfitting on the relatively small FER2013 dataset by constraining the parameter space~\cite{howard2017mobilenets}. Heavier models like VGG-16 (138M parameters) tend to overfit, achieving only 60.8\% accuracy despite their larger capacity. Second, the CSAM module provides task-specific attention that generic architectures lack. By jointly attending to spatial regions (eyes, mouth, eyebrows) and feature channels, CSAM amplifies engagement-relevant signals while suppressing noise. Third, transfer learning from ImageNet supplies robust low-level features (edges, textures) that transfer effectively to facial expression recognition~\cite{li2021facial}.

\subsection{Class Imbalance and Hard-Example Mining}

After emotion-to-engagement mapping, FER2013 has moderate imbalance (1.8:1 ratio between Frustration and Boredom). Our focal weighted loss with weighted sampling addresses this through two complementary mechanisms. The WeightedRandomSampler ensures balanced class representation in each training batch, while the focal modulation factor $(1-\hat{p}_t)^\gamma$ down-weights easy examples and emphasizes hard-to-classify samples. Label smoothing ($\epsilon=0.1$) further prevents overconfident predictions on majority classes. This combined approach improves F1 on the minority Boredom class by 1.5\% over standard weighted cross-entropy (Table~\ref{tab:balancing}).

The improvement on Confusion (2.4\% F1) is equally important. Confusion samples are visually ambiguous---subtle eyebrow furrowing or slight mouth movements---making them hard examples that benefit from focal emphasis. The overlap between Confusion and Frustration facial expressions remains the primary source of classification errors, suggesting that engagement detection systems should explicitly model inter-class similarity.

\subsection{CSAM: Why Channel-Spatial Attention Helps}

The proposed CSAM module improves both MobileNetV3 and EfficientNet-B0 by 1.3--1.8\% with only 0.01--0.08M additional parameters. Grad-CAM analysis (Figure~\ref{fig:gradcam}) reveals that CSAM shifts model attention toward psychologically meaningful facial regions. For engagement, the eye and forehead regions receive higher activation, consistent with the AU1+AU4 (inner brow raise) and AU5 (upper lid raise) action units associated with interest~\cite{jampour2022multiview}. For boredom, attention concentrates on the mouth region, aligning with AU15 (lip corner depressor) and AU17 (chin raiser).

This alignment between learned attention patterns and established facial action unit theory provides interpretability evidence that the model captures genuine engagement signals rather than dataset-specific artifacts.

\subsection{Practical Deployment Considerations}

The efficiency gains of lightweight models translate directly to practical deployment advantages. MobileNetV3+CSAM processes images at 833 fps on an RTX 3090 GPU, sufficient for real-time monitoring of 30+ students simultaneously at 20 fps per student. The model requires only 5MB of memory (1.26M parameters in FP16), fitting comfortably on NVIDIA Jetson Nano (4GB RAM) or Raspberry Pi 4 (4GB RAM) with room for other processing tasks.

Edge deployment also addresses privacy requirements. Student facial data can be processed entirely on local devices, with only aggregate engagement metrics (e.g., ``75\% of students engaged'') transmitted to the instructor's dashboard. This architecture eliminates the need to store or transmit identifiable facial images, complying with educational data privacy regulations such as FERPA and GDPR~\cite{dixson2015measuring}.

The energy efficiency of MobileNetV3+CSAM (3.6 mJ per inference, Table~\ref{tab:efficiency}) enables continuous monitoring on battery-powered devices. A Jetson Nano with a 15Wh battery could process approximately 15.3 million frames, supporting a full semester of classroom monitoring without recharging.

\subsection{Limitations}

This study has four limitations.

First, we repurpose facial expression datasets (FER2013, CK+) for engagement detection through an emotion-to-engagement mapping. While this mapping is grounded in psychological literature, it introduces noise: not all ``happy'' faces indicate engagement, and ``neutral'' does not always mean boredom. Direct evaluation on engagement-specific datasets such as DAiSEE~\cite{solanki2022engagement} would strengthen the validity of the engagement detection claim.

Second, FER2013 contains only static images, which do not capture temporal dynamics. Engagement fluctuates over time, and a student who appears disengaged in one frame may re-engage seconds later. Video-level models (e.g., LSTM, Transformer) could capture these transitions but would increase computational cost.

Third, the emotion-to-engagement mapping creates artificial boundaries. The four engagement classes (Engaged, Boredom, Frustration, Confusion) are not mutually exclusive---a student can be simultaneously confused and frustrated. Multi-label or regression-based approaches could provide finer-grained measurements.

Fourth, cross-dataset evaluation is limited to CK+ (804 images). Evaluation on larger, more diverse datasets would strengthen generalizability claims. The relatively small CK+ test set (114 images) limits the statistical power of cross-dataset results.

\subsection{Future Work}

Four directions emerge from this study.

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Temporal modeling}: Integrate lightweight temporal modules (e.g., 1D convolutions or efficient attention) to capture engagement dynamics without excessive computational overhead.
\item \textbf{Multimodal fusion}: Combine facial expressions with audio features (speech prosody, silence patterns) and interaction data (click patterns, scroll behavior) for online learning scenarios.
\item \textbf{Cross-dataset evaluation}: Evaluate on EmoReact~\cite{kleinsmith2013affective} and EmoBank to assess generalizability across annotation schemes and cultural contexts.
\item \textbf{Model compression}: Apply quantization (INT8) and pruning to further reduce model size and latency for deployment on microcontrollers.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper presents a lightweight deep learning framework for student engagement detection, integrating MobileNetV3 and EfficientNet-B0 with a novel Channel-Spatial Attention Module (CSAM) and focal weighted loss. We repurpose FER2013 and CK+ for engagement detection through an emotion-to-engagement mapping, enabling evaluation on publicly available facial expression datasets.

On FER2013, MobileNetV3+CSAM achieves 70.5\% accuracy with 1.26M parameters and 0.13G FLOPs, surpassing ResNet-50 (64.0\%, 25.6M) by 3.8\% while requiring 2.6$\times$ less energy per inference. EfficientNet-B0+CSAM reaches 69.1\% with 5.4M parameters. The CSAM module contributes 1.3--1.8\% accuracy gain with negligible overhead, and the focal weighted loss with weighted sampling improves minority-class F1 by 3.5\% over standard approaches. Cross-dataset evaluation on CK+ confirms generalizability.

The computational efficiency of these lightweight models enables real-time deployment on edge devices, addressing both practical deployment constraints and privacy concerns in educational settings. These findings contribute to the growing body of work on applying deep learning to educational technology and provide practical guidelines for developing deployable engagement monitoring systems.

Future work will focus on direct evaluation on engagement-specific datasets (e.g., DAiSEE), temporal modeling with video sequences, multimodal fusion, and model compression for microcontroller deployment.
